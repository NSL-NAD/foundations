# Wind & Natural Ventilation

Wind is an invisible design partner. When you understand how air moves and learn to guide it through a building, you can cool rooms, improve air quality, and create spaces that feel alive and connected to the outdoors - all without relying on air conditioning.

## Cross Ventilation

Cross ventilation is the simplest and most effective natural cooling strategy. It works by placing openings on opposite or adjacent walls so that wind enters on one side and exits on the other, pulling fresh air through the entire room.

For cross ventilation to work well, keep these guidelines in mind:

- **Inlet and outlet openings** should be on opposite walls when possible.
- **The outlet should be slightly larger** than the inlet, which accelerates airflow through the space.
- **Avoid placing obstructions** like tall furniture or solid partitions between the inlet and outlet.
- **Room depth matters.** Cross ventilation is most effective when the distance between openings is no more than about five times the ceiling height.

## Stack Effect

Even without wind, you can ventilate a building using the stack effect. The principle is simple: warm air rises. If you provide an opening low on the building where cool air can enter and another opening high up where warm air can escape, you create a natural convection loop that continuously refreshes the air inside.

Architects use this strategy with:

- **Double-height spaces and atriums** that allow warm air to collect at the top and vent out through high windows or roof openings.
- **Stairwells and vertical shafts** that act as natural chimneys, drawing air upward.
- **Operable skylights and clerestory windows** that release the hottest air at the highest point.

The stack effect works best in climates with significant temperature differences between indoors and outdoors and in buildings with enough vertical height to generate meaningful airflow.

## Prevailing Winds

Every site has a dominant wind direction that varies by season. Before you begin designing, find out which way the prevailing winds blow on your site. Local weather data, wind roses, and even simple observation can reveal this information.

Once you know the prevailing wind direction:

- **Orient your building's main openings** to face into the breeze during warm months.
- **Use landscape elements** like trees, hedges, and berms to redirect or buffer wind as needed.
- **Plan your outdoor spaces** so that patios, porches, and gathering areas benefit from cooling breezes rather than being exposed to harsh gusts.

## Thermal Comfort

Moving air cools the body by accelerating evaporation from the skin. Even a gentle breeze can make a room feel several degrees cooler than the actual air temperature. Conversely, stagnant air traps heat and moisture around occupants, creating discomfort even at moderate temperatures.

This is why ceiling fans, operable windows, and thoughtful ventilation design have such a significant impact on how comfortable a space feels. You do not always need to lower the air temperature; sometimes you just need to move the air.
