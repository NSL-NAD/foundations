# Texture and Materiality

## Definition

Texture is the tangible surface quality of materials: rough or smooth, warm or cool, matte or reflective. Materiality extends beyond the tactile to include the emotional and cultural connotations that materials carry. Together, texture and materiality shape how a space feels to the body and the mind.

## Why It Matters

Texture connects users physically and emotionally to a space. Architecture is not just something you look at; it is something you touch, walk on, lean against, and feel the temperature of. The grain of a wooden handrail under your palm, the coolness of a stone floor beneath bare feet, the softness of a plaster wall catching warm light -- these sensory experiences are often what people remember most about a space, even if they cannot articulate why.

## The Sensory Experience of Materials

Every material has a personality, shaped by its physical properties and cultural associations:

- **Stone** feels solid, permanent, and grounded. It carries associations of durability and tradition. Its surface can range from rough-hewn and rustic to polished and refined.
- **Wood** feels warm, natural, and inviting. It ages gracefully, developing a patina over time that deepens its character. Different species offer vastly different textures, from the smooth grain of maple to the rough, knotted surface of reclaimed barn wood.
- **Concrete** can feel raw and industrial or sleek and modern, depending on its finish. Board-formed concrete retains the texture of the wooden formwork, creating a dialogue between two materials.
- **Metal** feels precise, cool, and contemporary. Steel, copper, bronze, and aluminum each have distinct visual and tactile qualities, and many metals change color and texture as they weather.
- **Glass** feels open, transparent, and light. It dissolves the boundary between inside and outside, but it can also feel cold and impersonal if overused.
- **Fabric and soft materials** introduce warmth, sound absorption, and a sense of comfort that hard surfaces cannot provide.

## Visual Texture vs. Tactile Texture

Texture operates at two scales. Tactile texture is what you feel when you touch a surface: the roughness of brick, the smoothness of marble. Visual texture is what you perceive from a distance: the pattern of a stone wall, the grain of a wooden panel, the ripple of a corrugated metal facade. Both scales matter. A building might look smooth from across the street but reveal rich, complex textures as you approach and touch it.

## Material Honesty

Many architects value the idea of material honesty, letting materials appear as what they are rather than disguising them. Exposed brick, visible wood grain, and raw concrete all reflect this philosophy. When materials are honest, the building tells a story about how it was made and what it is made of.
