# Context and Site

## Definition

Context is the full set of surroundings that a building exists within: the landscape, the climate, the existing built environment, the cultural history, and the social life of a place. Site is the specific piece of ground a building occupies. Together, context and site shape every meaningful architectural decision, from orientation and materials to form and scale.

## Why It Matters

Architecture does not exist in a vacuum. A building must harmonize with, or thoughtfully contrast, its environment to feel authentic and appropriate. A beach house that ignores the ocean, a mountain cabin that fights the slope, or an urban building that turns its back on the street -- all of these feel wrong, even to someone who has never studied architecture. Context is what keeps a building grounded in reality.

## Responding to Place

Every site has unique qualities that should inform the design:

- **Topography** -- Is the ground flat, sloped, or terraced? The shape of the land suggests how a building should sit: nestled into a hillside, raised on stilts above a floodplain, or spread across a level plain.
- **Orientation** -- Where is north? Where does the sun rise and set? Which direction do the prevailing winds come from? These factors determine window placement, shading strategies, and outdoor space locations.
- **Views** -- What is worth looking at, and what should be screened? A coastal site might open up entirely toward the water while closing off a less appealing inland direction.
- **Vegetation and landscape** -- Existing trees, gardens, and natural features are assets. Good design preserves and responds to them rather than clearing everything away.

## Responding to Climate

Climate is one of the most fundamental aspects of context. Architecture that works beautifully in a hot, dry desert would be deeply uncomfortable in a cold, wet forest. Climate-responsive design considers:

- **Temperature** -- Insulation, thermal mass, ventilation, and shading all respond to local temperature patterns.
- **Rainfall** -- Roof slopes, overhangs, drainage, and material choices all depend on how much water falls and how often.
- **Wind** -- Prevailing winds can be harnessed for natural ventilation or blocked to protect outdoor spaces.
- **Sunlight** -- The intensity and angle of sunlight vary enormously by latitude and season, affecting everything from window sizing to wall color.

## Responding to Neighborhood

Buildings exist among other buildings. The scale, materials, setbacks, and character of the surrounding neighborhood all matter. A new building can echo the patterns of its neighbors, creating continuity and a sense of belonging. Or it can contrast with them deliberately, introducing something new and unexpected. Both approaches can succeed, but both require a deep understanding of what is already there.
