# Form

## Definition

Form is the visible shape and configuration of a building or object. It is what gives space its character and identity. When you look at a building from the outside, the form is the first thing you perceive: its silhouette against the sky, the geometry of its walls and roof, the overall mass of the structure.

## Why It Matters

Form evokes emotional responses and creates first impressions. A sharp, angular building feels aggressive and bold. A curved, flowing structure feels soft and organic. A simple rectangular box can feel calm and rational. Before anyone steps inside, the form of a building has already communicated something about what the experience will be.

Architects use form deliberately to express ideas, respond to context, and shape the way people feel about a place.

## Types of Form

Architectural forms generally fall into three broad categories:

### Rectilinear Forms

These are composed of straight lines and right angles: cubes, rectangles, and boxes. Rectilinear forms dominate most of the built environment because they are structurally efficient and easy to construct. They tend to feel orderly, stable, and rational. Think of a classic brick row house or a modernist glass tower.

### Curvilinear Forms

These use curves, arcs, and circular geometries: domes, cylinders, vaults, and swooping facades. Curvilinear forms introduce a sense of movement and fluidity. They can feel grand and monumental, like the dome of the Pantheon, or playful and dynamic, like the spiraling ramps of the Guggenheim Museum in New York.

### Organic Forms

Organic forms take their cues from nature. They are irregular, asymmetric, and often complex. These shapes resist simple geometric description and instead feel as though they grew rather than were built. Organic forms can create a powerful connection between a building and its natural surroundings.

## Form and Space

Form and space are inseparable. The exterior form of a building is the shell that contains interior space. When an architect manipulates form, they are simultaneously shaping the spaces within. A vaulted ceiling creates a different interior experience than a flat one, even if the floor area is identical.

This relationship means that form is never purely decorative. Every curve, angle, and surface has consequences for the space it encloses and the way people experience that space.
