## The Disclaimer I Lead With

I'm not a licensed architect. I don't have an architecture degree. I've never passed the ARE exams. If someone asks me to stamp a set of construction documents, I'm going to politely decline and recommend they call an actual architect.

I say this upfront because honesty matters — especially when you're building an architecture course for beginners and charging money for it. You deserve to know who's teaching you, what qualifies them, and where their knowledge comes from.

So here's the story.

---

## How an Engineer Fell in Love with Architecture

I'm an engineer by training. I spent years working with systems, structures, and problem-solving frameworks. Engineering taught me how to break complex things into understandable pieces, how to communicate technical concepts in plain language, and how to think about how things are built.

Then I decided to design my own home.

I started where everyone starts — browsing Pinterest, saving floor plans, watching YouTube tours of houses I couldn't afford. But the more I dug in, the more I realized I didn't actually understand what I was looking at. I could tell when a house *felt* right, but I couldn't explain why. I didn't know what made one floor plan flow and another feel awkward. I couldn't tell if a room was well-proportioned or just well-photographed.

So I started learning. I read everything I could find. I took [Harvard's free architecture course](https://pll.harvard.edu/course/architectural-imagination) online. I studied the fundamentals — proportion, scale, light, materials, spatial relationships. I pored over project studies on [ArchDaily](https://www.archdaily.com), not just admiring the photos but reading the architect's design rationale.

And something clicked. Architecture isn't magic. It's not a gift that some people have and others don't. It's a set of principles — learnable, teachable principles — that explain why some spaces make you feel alive and others make you want to leave.

---

## The Gap I Kept Running Into

Here's the thing that frustrated me throughout that learning process: almost everything available was designed for architecture students or practicing professionals. The textbooks assumed you knew what a section drawing was. The online courses assumed you could already read a floor plan. The terminology was dense, the explanations were academic, and the whole thing felt like a closed club with an unspoken entrance requirement.

I don't think that's intentional. Architects are generous with knowledge — I've met many who will happily explain anything you ask. But the formal educational resources? They're built for people who are already inside the profession.

What about the rest of us? What about the homeowner who wants to understand what their architect is proposing? The person saving up for a custom build who wants to walk into that first meeting with real knowledge? The self-taught designer who learns by doing?

That gap is why Foundations of Architecture exists. I built the architecture course for beginners that I wish had existed when I started — one that assumes you know nothing, explains everything, and gives you real skills you can use immediately.

---

## Why Not Being an Architect Is Actually an Advantage

This might sound like cope, but hear me out.

Licensed architects have spent years inside architectural education. They've internalized concepts so deeply that they sometimes forget those concepts need explaining. The [curse of knowledge](https://www.archdaily.com) is real — the better you know something, the harder it is to remember what it was like not knowing it.

I remember exactly what it was like not knowing. I remember staring at a floor plan and not understanding why the walls had different thicknesses. I remember confusing a section with an elevation. I remember the first time someone explained [architectural scale](/blog/what-is-architectural-scale) to me and the lightbulb that went on.

Every lesson in FOA Course is built from the perspective of someone who had to learn these concepts from scratch, as an adult, with no formal training. I know where the confusion points are because I hit every single one of them. I know which explanations work because I tested dozens that didn't.

That's not a replacement for deep professional expertise. But for the specific audience I'm teaching — homeowners, aspiring self-builders, curious learners — it's exactly the right perspective.

## What Qualifies Me (Honestly)

Let me lay it out plainly:

**What I bring:** An engineering mindset. Thousands of hours studying architectural fundamentals. Real experience designing a home from the ground up. The ability to explain complex concepts in language anyone can follow. A genuine obsession with making architecture accessible.

**What I don't bring:** A professional license. The ability to design a structurally sound building from scratch. Years of practice managing construction projects. The deep intuition that comes from decades of professional design work.

FOA Course is not a replacement for hiring an architect. I say this in the course itself, multiple times. The goal isn't to turn you into a professional — it's to turn you into an informed, confident participant in the design of your own home. Those are very different things, and the distinction matters.

If you're planning to build, you should hire an architect. But you should hire one as an educated client, not a passive one. That's what this course gives you.
