## Everyone Starts in the Same Place: "I Want to Build My Dream Home"

That sentence — "I want to build my dream home" — is where every FOA student begins. And if I'm honest, it's also where most people get stuck. Because wanting a dream home and knowing how to **plan your dream home** are two completely different skills.

The wanting part is easy. You've been collecting inspiration for years. You know you want natural light. You know you want a big kitchen. You know you want... something. But when it comes time to turn those feelings into decisions, the process suddenly feels overwhelming. What size should the house be? Which way should it face? What's a realistic budget? How do you communicate all of this to an architect without sounding clueless?

I built the FOA Course specifically to bridge that gap. Not to turn non-architects into architects — that takes seven years and a lot more money than $93. But to give you the vocabulary, the framework, and the confidence to go from "I want" to "here's what I need, and here's why."

Let me walk you through how that journey actually works.

## Phase 1: Defining What "Dream Home" Actually Means to You

This is where most people want to skip ahead to floor plans. Don't. The first phase of the home design process is about getting ruthlessly honest about how you live, what you value, and what trade-offs you're willing to make.

In the course, we start with what I call the Vision Module. It asks questions most people have never considered. Not "how many bedrooms?" — that comes later. But:

How do you spend a typical Saturday morning? Where does your family naturally gather? What room in your current home do you avoid, and why? What does "privacy" mean to you? How do you feel about maintaining a large yard?

These aren't fluffy journaling prompts. They're design drivers. The answers directly inform decisions about layout, orientation, room sizes, and even architectural style. If you haven't read our article on the [7 questions to answer before designing your dream home](https://foacourse.com/blog/7-questions-before-designing-dream-home), start there — it gives you a preview of this thinking.

By the end of this phase, students have a clear lifestyle profile that serves as the foundation for every decision that follows. It's the difference between designing a home that looks good on paper and designing one that actually works for the people inside it.

---

## Phase 2: Building Your Design Brief

The design brief is, hands down, the most valuable document a future homeowner can create. It's a structured summary of your requirements, preferences, and priorities — organized in a format that any architect or designer can immediately understand and work from.

Think of it as a dream home blueprint for your *intentions*, created before anyone draws a single line.

In the course, we build the design brief systematically. Students work through:

**Space requirements.** Not just a room list, but a prioritized room list. What's essential? What's nice to have? What would you cut if the budget tightens? Every room gets a size range, an adjacency preference (what should it be near?), and a brief description of how it'll be used.

**Site considerations.** Orientation preferences, views worth capturing, outdoor spaces, relationship to the street, privacy needs. If you already have a lot, these are specific. If you're still looking, they become criteria for your land search.

**Style direction.** Not just "I like modern farmhouse" but why. What specific qualities attract you? The warmth of natural materials? The drama of high ceilings and glass? Understanding the *why* behind your aesthetic preferences helps architects design something that feels authentically you rather than a copy of someone else's Pinterest board.

**Budget parameters.** Realistic ranges for construction cost, professional fees, site work, and contingency. I explain how to think about [what it costs to build a house](https://foacourse.com/blog/how-much-does-it-cost-to-build-a-house-2026) in another article, and the course goes deeper — helping you align your ambitions with your budget before you discover the gap the expensive way.

**Non-negotiables and deal-breakers.** Every student identifies their absolute musts and absolute won'ts. These become guard rails that keep the project on track when the inevitable scope creep starts whispering "what if we just added a..."

The design brief typically ends up being three to five pages. Students who go on to hire architects consistently report that handing over this document transforms their first meeting. Instead of an hour of vague conversation, they walk in prepared. The architect knows they're serious, knows they've done their homework, and can immediately start adding professional value instead of spending time extracting basic information.

## Phase 3: Learning the Language

You can't effectively participate in the home design process if you don't understand the vocabulary. This is where architectural literacy comes in — and it's where the FOA Course does something most resources don't: teaches you to *read* architecture, not just admire it.

Students learn to understand [floor plans](https://foacourse.com/blog/what-is-a-floor-plan) — not just what rooms are labeled, but how to evaluate traffic flow, spatial proportions, sight lines, and adjacencies. They learn what [architectural scale](https://foacourse.com/blog/what-is-architectural-scale) means and how to measure drawings. They learn about structural basics like load-bearing walls, foundation types, and why some design ideas are straightforward while others require significant engineering.

This literacy phase isn't about becoming an expert. It's about becoming conversant. When your architect says "we could do a clerestory here," you don't need to design a clerestory — you just need to know it means high windows that bring in light without sacrificing wall space, and you need to know whether that sounds good for your situation.

As <a href="https://www.archdaily.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">ArchDaily</a> has observed, client-architect communication is one of the most significant factors in project success. Educating homeowners in architectural fundamentals directly improves design outcomes. That's not a soft benefit. It translates to fewer revisions, less frustration, and a better final product.

## Phase 4: Testing Your Ideas

This is where things get fun. With a design brief in hand and architectural literacy under their belt, students start testing their ideas spatially.

Some students use SketchUp Free to build rough 3D models. Others work with bubble diagrams — simple circles representing rooms, connected by lines showing relationships. Others use graph paper and a ruler. The tool doesn't matter. What matters is translating the written brief into spatial relationships.

"I want the kitchen near the entry so I can unload groceries easily." Great — that's a spatial decision. Put those bubbles next to each other.

"I want the master bedroom away from the kids' rooms for sound privacy." That's another spatial decision. Separate those bubbles with buffer spaces.

"I want to see the backyard from the kitchen sink." That's an orientation decision. The kitchen goes on the side of the house facing the backyard.

These exercises surface conflicts early. What happens when your desire for a kitchen near the entry conflicts with your desire for a kitchen facing the backyard — and the entry is on the front? Now you're problem-solving, and now you understand why architects earn their fees.

Students don't produce construction documents in this phase. They produce informed concepts — layouts that demonstrate clear thinking about priorities and trade-offs. They understand their own project well enough to evaluate an architect's proposals critically, ask smart questions, and make decisions confidently.
