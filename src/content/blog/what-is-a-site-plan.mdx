## Your Property Is More Than a Rectangle on a Map

Picture this: you just closed on a beautiful half-acre lot. Rolling terrain, mature trees, a view of the hills to the west. You're already imagining where the kitchen will face, where the patio will go, where your kids will play. You hire an architect, hand them your Pinterest board, and say "let's go."

And the first thing they ask is: "Do you have a site plan?"

A **site plan** is a scaled drawing of your property that shows the boundaries, existing features, legal restrictions, and physical conditions of your land. Think of it as the X-ray of your lot. It reveals everything hiding beneath the surface — both literally and legally — that will shape what you can and can't build.

Here's the thing: your dream home doesn't exist in a vacuum. It exists on a specific piece of land, subject to specific rules, with specific challenges. A site plan is how you understand all of that *before* you start designing.

---

## What Does a Site Plan Actually Show?

A site plan packs a surprising amount of information into one document. Let's break down the key elements you'll find on a typical property site plan.

### Property Boundaries and Dimensions

This is the most basic layer — the exact legal boundaries of your lot, with precise measurements. These come from a professional land survey and tell you exactly how much land you own. Not what the fence line suggests. Not what your neighbor told you. The actual, legally recorded boundaries.

You'd be amazed how often fence lines don't match property lines. I've seen disputes over a few feet of land hold up entire projects. Get this right first.

### Setbacks

Setbacks are the minimum distances your building must maintain from property lines, roads, and other features. Your local zoning code dictates these, and they're non-negotiable.

A typical residential lot might have a 25-foot front setback, 10-foot side setbacks, and a 20-foot rear setback. Once you draw those lines on your site plan, the buildable area — the envelope where your house can actually go — is often much smaller than you expected.

I covered setbacks and other zoning restrictions in the [building codes article](https://foacourse.com/blog/what-are-building-codes), and I'd strongly recommend reading that alongside this one. The site plan is where those codes become visual and real.

### Easements

An easement is a legal right for someone else to use a portion of your property for a specific purpose. Common examples include utility easements (where power lines or sewer pipes run), drainage easements, and access easements (where a neighbor has a right to cross your land to reach theirs).

You can't build permanent structures on most easements. So that dream detached garage? If there's a 15-foot utility easement running right through where you planned to put it, you're going back to the drawing board. A site plan for building any structure needs to show every easement clearly.

### Existing Utilities

Your site plan should mark the locations of water lines, sewer connections (or septic systems), gas lines, electrical service, and stormwater infrastructure. Knowing where these are tells your architect and builder where to connect your new home — and where NOT to dig.

Hitting a gas line because nobody checked the site plan is the kind of mistake that makes the evening news. Don't be that story.

### Topography and Grading

Topography is the shape of your land — the hills, slopes, and flat areas described through contour lines on the site plan. Each contour line represents a specific elevation, and the spacing between them tells you how steep the terrain is. Lines close together mean steep. Lines far apart mean gentle.

Why does this matter? Water flows downhill. If your lot slopes toward where you want to build, you need a drainage strategy. If there's a significant grade change, you might need a walkout basement, retaining walls, or creative foundation work. The topography drives more design decisions than most people realize.

### Existing Structures and Features

Trees, outbuildings, driveways, wells, septic fields, retaining walls, fences — everything that currently exists on the property gets documented. Many municipalities have tree preservation ordinances that prevent you from removing trees above a certain diameter without a permit. Your site plan captures all of this.

### North Arrow and Sun Path

This small detail has massive design implications. Knowing which direction is north tells your architect where the sun rises, where it sets, and how light moves across your property throughout the day and across seasons. Passive solar design, window placement, outdoor living spaces — all of these depend on orientation.

## Why a Site Plan Matters Before Design Begins

I'll be direct: designing a house without a site plan is like buying clothes without knowing your size. You might get lucky, but you'll probably end up with something that doesn't fit.

Here's what happens when people skip the site analysis architecture professionals rely on:

**They design a house that's too big for the buildable area.** Once setbacks and easements are accounted for, many lots have less usable space than owners assume. Better to know your constraints upfront than to redesign after you've fallen in love with a layout.

**They ignore drainage and end up with water problems.** A flat-looking lot might actually slope slightly toward the house location. Without topographic data, you won't catch this until you're mopping your basement.

**They place the house wrong on the lot.** Orientation affects natural light, energy efficiency, privacy, views, and the usability of outdoor spaces. A site plan gives you the information to make these decisions intentionally rather than accidentally.

**They discover easements or restrictions after submitting plans.** Nothing kills momentum like having your building permit rejected because your design encroaches on an easement you didn't know about. Your permitting department will check — even if you didn't.

According to <a href="https://www.archdaily.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">ArchDaily</a>, site analysis is considered one of the foundational steps in the architectural design process. It's not optional. It's not something you do later. It's step one.

## How to Get a Site Plan

There are a few ways to get a site plan, and the right approach depends on what you already have and what your project requires.

### Option 1: Check Your County Records

Many counties keep plat maps and recorded surveys on file. You can often access these through your county assessor's website or by visiting the clerk's office. These won't have topography or detailed existing conditions, but they'll give you boundaries, dimensions, and recorded easements.

This is a good free starting point, but it's usually not sufficient for design or permitting.

### Option 2: Hire a Licensed Land Surveyor

For any serious building project, you need a professional survey. A licensed surveyor will come to your property, set up equipment, and produce an accurate, legally defensible document showing boundaries, elevations, existing features, and easements.

Expect to pay between $500 and $2,000 depending on the size and complexity of your lot. Wooded lots, steep terrain, or properties with unclear boundaries cost more.

This is money well spent. The survey protects you legally and gives your design team the foundation they need to do good work.

### Option 3: Request One from Your Builder or Architect

If you've already hired a design professional, they'll likely coordinate the survey for you. Many architects won't begin schematic design until they have a current survey in hand — and the good ones will insist on it.

The <a href="https://www.nahb.org" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">National Association of Home Builders (NAHB)</a> recommends that homeowners obtain a property survey early in the planning process, well before breaking ground.

## What About Existing Homes?

If you're renovating or adding onto an existing home, you still need a site plan. An addition has to comply with the same setback and easement rules as new construction. A deck, a detached garage, even a significant fence — many of these require a property site plan as part of the permit application.

If your home was built recently, the original builder may have commissioned a survey that's still on file. Check with your title company or county records office.

For older homes, you'll likely need a new survey, especially if you're making changes near property lines.

## Reading a Site Plan: Tips for Non-Architects

You don't need a degree to read a site plan. Here are a few things to look for:

**Scale and north arrow.** Every site plan should have both. The scale tells you how to measure distances on the drawing. The north arrow orients you.

**The buildable area.** Mentally (or physically) draw the setback lines inside your property boundaries. The remaining rectangle — or whatever shape it forms — is where your house can go.

**Contour lines.** Remember: close together means steep, far apart means flat. Look for how water would flow across your lot naturally.

**Utility connection points.** Find where water, sewer, and electrical service enter your property. These affect where your house connects to infrastructure and can influence layout decisions.

**Trees and features worth preserving.** Large trees, rock formations, views — mark the things you want to keep. These become design assets rather than obstacles.

## The Site Plan Sets the Stage for Everything

A site plan isn't glamorous. Nobody puts their site plan on Instagram. But it's the single most important document in the early stages of any building project. It tells you what you're working with, what the rules are, and where the opportunities lie.

Every design decision that follows — from the footprint of your house to the placement of your front door — traces back to what the site plan revealed.
