## Two Styles, One Question: Which One Is Actually *You*?

You're scrolling through Houzz or Pinterest, saving images of dream homes, and you start to notice a pattern. Half your saves look like they belong on a countryside estate with shiplap walls and a wraparound porch. The other half look like they were teleported from a design magazine — all glass, steel, and razor-sharp rooflines.

Welcome to the **modern farmhouse vs contemporary** dilemma. These are arguably the two most popular residential architectural styles in America right now, and they couldn't be more different in personality. But here's the thing: choosing between them isn't about which one is "better." It's about which one matches how you actually live.

Let me break down both styles so you can stop saving contradictory images and start designing with clarity.

---

## What Makes Modern Farmhouse Design So Popular?

The modern farmhouse is essentially a love letter to rural American architecture, updated with contemporary amenities and cleaner proportions. It takes the warmth and nostalgia of a traditional farmhouse — the big porch, the pitched roof, the connection to the land — and strips away the clutter.

### Key Features of Modern Farmhouse Design

**Exterior.** Board and batten siding (those vertical boards with narrow strips covering the joints) is the signature move. You'll also see standing seam metal roofs, gabled rooflines, large covered porches, and a restrained color palette — usually white, black, and natural wood tones. Windows tend to be tall and divided into panes, giving that classic grid pattern.

**Materials.** Wood is king. Reclaimed barn wood, shiplap paneling, butcher block countertops, wide-plank hardwood floors. Modern farmhouse design leans heavily on natural materials that feel tactile and lived-in. You'll see matte black hardware, open shelving in kitchens, and farmhouse sinks that could double as bathtubs.

**Layout.** Open floor plans are almost universal in modern farmhouse homes. The kitchen flows into the living room, which flows into the dining area. Mudrooms are a staple — this is a style that acknowledges people track dirt inside. And there's almost always a large island in the kitchen, because the kitchen is where everybody ends up anyway.

**Feeling.** Cozy. Approachable. Like a hug from a house. The modern farmhouse says "come in, kick off your shoes, stay a while." It's the architectural equivalent of a flannel shirt — comfortable and unpretentious.

As <a href="https://www.dwell.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Dwell</a> has noted, the modern farmhouse trend has shown remarkable staying power, evolving beyond a passing fad into a genuine design movement that continues to influence both custom homes and production builders.

## What Defines Contemporary Home Design?

Here's where I need to make an important distinction that gets architectural styles explained properly. "Contemporary" and "modern" are not the same thing. Modern architecture refers to a specific historical movement from the early-to-mid 20th century (think Frank Lloyd Wright, Le Corbusier). Contemporary means "of the current time." It's a living, evolving style that borrows from multiple influences.

That said, when most people say "contemporary home design," they're picturing something specific.

### Key Features of Contemporary Home Design

**Exterior.** Flat or low-slope roofs, asymmetrical forms, large expanses of glass, and mixed materials — think concrete, steel, wood, and stone used together in deliberate contrast. The silhouette is horizontal and grounded. Color palettes tend toward grays, charcoals, natural wood, and white, but with more freedom to introduce bold accent colors than farmhouse allows.

**Materials.** Contemporary homes embrace a wider material palette. Exposed concrete, cor-ten steel (that beautiful rusted-looking metal), floor-to-ceiling glass, polished concrete floors, and engineered surfaces. The finishes are often sleek — flat panel cabinetry, integrated appliances, handleless drawers. Everything feels intentional and edited.

**Layout.** Contemporary homes also favor open layouts, but with more spatial variety. You might find a dramatic double-height living space that opens to a mezzanine, or rooms defined by partial walls and level changes rather than traditional walls and doors. Indoor-outdoor flow is prioritized through sliding glass walls and courtyard designs.

**Feeling.** Curated. Confident. Like the house has an opinion about itself. Contemporary design says "I've been considered." It rewards you with dramatic moments — a framed view, a shaft of light across a concrete wall, a staircase that feels like sculpture.

## Side-by-Side: Where They Differ

Let me lay out the practical differences, because this is where the decision gets real.

### Rooflines

Modern farmhouse: Steeply pitched gable roofs, often with multiple gables. The roof IS the design.

Contemporary: Flat, butterfly, or low-slope roofs. The roof disappears or becomes a horizontal plane. In snowy climates, flat roofs need careful engineering — something to discuss with your architect.

### Windows

Modern farmhouse: Grid-patterned windows, often double-hung. Thoughtful but traditional in proportion.

Contemporary: Large, uninterrupted panes. Floor-to-ceiling glass is common. Minimal frames. The goal is to dissolve the boundary between inside and outside.

### Color and Texture

Modern farmhouse: Limited palette. White, black, and warm natural wood. Texture comes from material variety — rough wood against smooth metal, woven textiles against painted surfaces.

Contemporary: More chromatic range, but still restrained. Texture comes from material contrast — smooth concrete against warm wood, polished steel against raw stone.

### Warmth vs. Drama

This is the big one. Modern farmhouse design optimizes for warmth. Every material choice, every design decision asks "does this feel inviting?" Contemporary home design optimizes for impact. It asks "does this create an experience?"

Neither approach is wrong. They just serve different emotional needs.

## Cost Considerations

Let's talk money, because style has a price tag.

**Modern farmhouse** tends to be slightly more cost-friendly for a few reasons. The materials are readily available. Board and batten siding, metal roofing, and standard window shapes are all common in the residential supply chain. Many production builders offer modern farmhouse plans, which means you can find semi-custom options that reduce design costs.

**Contemporary homes** typically cost more per square foot. Custom steel fabrication, large glass panels, flat roof assemblies, and specialty finishes all carry premiums. Contemporary design also demands more precise construction — when everything is clean lines and minimal trim, there's nowhere to hide imperfections. You need skilled trades.

That said, both styles exist across a wide price spectrum. A modest modern farmhouse and a modest contemporary home can cost similarly. The premium kicks in when you lean into the signature features of each style.

## Which Style Fits Your Life?

I'm not going to tell you which one to pick. But I will give you some questions to ask yourself.

**Do you want your home to feel like a retreat or a gallery?** If you come home and want to sink into comfort, farmhouse might be your lane. If you come home and want to feel inspired by your surroundings, contemporary might speak to you.

**How do you feel about maintenance?** White board and batten needs regular cleaning and repainting. Large glass expanses need regular washing. Both styles have maintenance demands — they're just different ones.

**What does your lot look like?** A wooded, rural lot might naturally complement a farmhouse aesthetic. An urban infill lot or a property with a dramatic view might call for a contemporary approach that frames the landscape. Your site often has opinions about style.

**How do you entertain?** Modern farmhouse kitchens are designed to be social command centers — everyone gathers at the island. Contemporary homes might create more varied entertaining zones — a conversation pit here, an outdoor room there, a dramatic bar area. Think about how you host.

**What's your relationship with stuff?** Contemporary design demands editing. It works best with less. If you have collections, artwork, and objects you love to display, modern farmhouse might give you more flexibility to layer those personal touches without fighting the architecture.

## The Hybrid Approach

Here's a secret that nobody in the design-blog world wants to admit: most people don't want pure anything. They want a modern farmhouse living room with a contemporary bathroom. They want board and batten on the outside with polished concrete on the inside.

And that's completely fine.

The best residential architects know how to blend these architectural styles explained above into something coherent that feels uniquely like you. The key is having a dominant language — pick one style as your foundation — and then borrowing accent moments from the other.

A farmhouse with oversized, un-gridded windows? Works beautifully. A contemporary home with a reclaimed wood feature wall? Stunning. The problems start when neither style dominates and the house feels confused rather than curated.

## Start with How You Live, Not How It Looks

The biggest mistake I see people make when choosing between **modern farmhouse vs contemporary** is starting with aesthetics and ignoring lifestyle. A style should serve your life, not the other way around.

Before you commit to board and batten or floor-to-ceiling glass, spend time thinking about your daily routines, your entertaining style, your tolerance for maintenance, and the feeling you want when you walk through your front door. The answers will point you toward the right style — or the right blend.
