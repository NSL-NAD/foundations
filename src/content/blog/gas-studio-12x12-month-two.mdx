## Four Down. Eight to Go.

A few weeks ago I wrote about the [GAS Studio 12x12 challenge](/blog/building-12-houses-12-months) — the commitment to launch 12 projects in 12 months through <a href="https://goodatscale.studio" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">GAS Studio</a>. The premise: leverage the tools and technology available right now to ship real things into the world. Apps, e-commerce, education, marketing — anything that creates value.

That post laid out the "why." This one is the honest progress report. Four projects have shipped. Here's what each one taught me and what's changing about the challenge as it unfolds.

---

## Project by Project: What Actually Happened

### Project 1: Margle

**What it is:** A web application.

**What I learned:** Scope kills speed. The first version of Margle had a feature list that would've taken three months to build. I cut it to the one thing the app needed to do well, built that, and launched. Two weeks later I added a second feature based on actual user behavior instead of what I imagined users would want.

This set the tone for the entire 12x12. Plan less, ship sooner, iterate based on reality.

### Project 2: FOA Course

**What it is:** The education platform you're reading this on — a 62-lesson architecture course with a student dashboard, Stripe checkout, and a blog publishing system.

**What I learned:** This was the hardest project by far. Not because the technology was complex (though it was), but because the content required genuine expertise and care. You can't rush curriculum design the way you can rush a landing page. Every lesson needed to be accurate, accessible, and useful.

The 12x12 constraint helped here in a counterintuitive way. Instead of trying to make every lesson perfect before launching, I focused on getting the course structure and core content solid, then committed to improving iteratively. The blog you're reading is part of that — a content engine that extends the course's value over time.

If the 12x12 hadn't forced a deadline, I'd probably still be reorganizing the module outline.

### Project 3: GAS Studio Website

**What it is:** The website for the design practice behind all of this.

**What I learned:** A live website you can improve is infinitely more valuable than a perfect website in a design file. I spent years with GAS Studio existing mostly as an idea and a logo. Giving it a real web presence in a month changed how I think about the practice itself. Shipping makes things real in a way that planning never does.

### Project 4: GAS Merch Store

**What it is:** An e-commerce store.

**What I learned:** I had zero e-commerce experience before this project. Setting up a brand, designing products, configuring fulfillment logistics, launching a storefront — all of it was new. And that was the most valuable part.

The 12x12 deliberately pushes me into domains I'm not comfortable in. Merch was the most foreign of the first four projects, and it taught me the most about areas I'd been ignoring: supply chain basics, product photography, conversion-focused design. None of this is architecture, but all of it makes me a more well-rounded creator — and a better teacher.

## The Tools Making This Possible

People keep asking how one person ships this many projects this fast. The honest answer: the tooling is extraordinary right now.

**AI-assisted development** has been the biggest multiplier. Not as a replacement for thinking, but as an accelerator for execution. When you know what you want to build, AI tools collapse the distance between intention and implementation. The FOA Course blog system, for example, went from concept to functioning pipeline in a fraction of the time it would've taken writing every line from scratch.

**Modern web frameworks** (Next.js, in our case) handle enormous amounts of complexity out of the box. Authentication, server-side rendering, API routes, image optimization — these used to be weeks of work each. Now they're configuration.

**No-code and low-code platforms** filled gaps where custom development wasn't worth the time. The merch store runs on infrastructure that would've required a full engineering team a decade ago.

**Managed hosting and CI/CD** mean I spend almost zero time on deployment and operations. Push code, it goes live. That sounds simple, but it's the kind of simple that only exists because the platforms are handling enormous complexity behind the scenes.

None of these tools do the thinking for you. Every project still required real decisions about scope, priorities, design, and quality. But the tools dramatically reduce the time between making a decision and seeing it in the world. That's the leverage that makes 12x12 possible.

## The Emotional Reality of Shipping Fast

I want to be honest about the harder parts.

**The novelty wore off.** Projects one and two were exciting because everything was new. By project four, the rhythm was familiar — and familiar meant the romantic energy of "I'm doing something bold" was replaced by "I have another deadline and I'm not sure this one's going to be good enough."

**Every project shipped with things I wanted to fix.** Every single one. There are features I cut, design decisions I compromised on, and entire sections I'd rebuild if I had another week. This is not comfortable for someone who cares about craft. But it's the trade-off the 12x12 demands: shipping with known imperfections is better than not shipping at all.

**Context-switching is exhausting.** Going from education platform to e-commerce store to web app requires a mental reset each time. Different tools, different audiences, different success metrics. The first week of each new project is the slowest because I'm re-orienting.

**But the compound effect is real.** Despite all of this, four projects into the challenge I feel sharper, faster, and more confident than I did at the start. Not because any individual project was a masterpiece, but because the act of shipping repeatedly builds a skillset that no amount of planning can replicate.

## How This Shapes the FOA Course

The FOA Course was project two of the 12x12, but the challenge continues to shape it.

**The "done beats perfect" lesson lives in the curriculum.** The course teaches architectural principles, but it also teaches a mindset: good fundamentals applied decisively produce better results than perfect knowledge applied hesitantly. Every 12x12 project reinforces this. When you're designing a home, you'll face hundreds of decisions — and the ability to make them confidently, informed by solid principles, is more valuable than agonizing over optimal solutions.

**The blog is a 12x12 product too.** The publishing system, the content calendar, the behind-the-scenes articles — this infrastructure was built under the same "ship it" constraints as everything else. And it directly benefits FOA students by providing a steady stream of practical content that extends beyond the core lessons.

**Real experience keeps the teaching honest.** If I were only teaching and never building, the course would slowly drift toward theory. The 12x12 keeps me grounded in the reality of making things — the deadlines, the compromises, the surprises. That experience translates directly into better, more practical education.
