## Thirty Days In, Everything Changed

If you read the [first GAS Studio 12x12 update](/blog/building-12-houses-12-months), you know the premise: design twelve residential projects in twelve months. One house per month. No excuses. The goal was to sharpen our design process, stress-test our tools, and generate real-world lessons that could feed directly into the FOA Course.

Month one was about momentum. Getting the system running, proving we could actually produce a complete residential concept in thirty days. It was messy and energizing and we pulled it off.

Month two was about confronting the things month one let us get away with.

---

## House #2: The Brief That Fought Back

The second house in our GAS Studio 12x12 challenge was supposed to be straightforward — a 1,600-square-foot, single-story home on a flat lot in a temperate climate. Three bedrooms, two baths, attached garage. The kind of house that gets built ten thousand times a year.

But I made a deliberate choice to add a constraint: the lot was narrow. Really narrow. Forty feet wide with five-foot side setbacks, leaving us thirty feet of buildable width. And suddenly "straightforward" became a puzzle.

Thirty feet of width changes *everything*. A standard two-car garage is twenty feet wide, which leaves only ten feet for the rest of the house. That doesn't work. So you go to a tandem garage (cars parked front to back), which solves the width problem but eats into the depth. Or you detach the garage, which costs more. Or you do a one-car garage and an uncovered parking pad.

This is the kind of trade-off that real homeowners face all the time — and it's exactly the kind of thing you don't think about until you're forced to. It became the first real design lesson of month two: constraints aren't obstacles. They're the entire point of design.

---

## The Design Decision That Changed the Plan

We went through four scheme options for House #2 before landing on one that worked. The first three kept trying to force a conventional room arrangement into an unconventional lot. The fourth one broke the rules.

Instead of a central hallway running the length of the house (which eats up precious width), we organized the plan around a single open living-kitchen-dining volume at the front and stacked the three bedrooms in a compact wing at the back. The hallway disappeared. A short passage connects the two zones, with a bathroom tucked on each side of it.

The result was a plan that felt bigger than 1,600 square feet because the primary living space was uninterrupted wall-to-wall. No hallway stealing width. No wasted circulation. The bedrooms were modest but well-proportioned — and because they were all on the same side of the house, plumbing was consolidated in a single wet wall. That's a real cost savings.

I'm not going to pretend this was a stroke of genius. It was the fourth attempt. The first three attempts all had that hallway, and I kept trying to make it work because hallways are what I'm used to drawing. The lesson — which absolutely became course content — is that the conventional approach is often just the first approach. Not the best one. It takes discipline to throw out what "should" work and find what actually does.

## Tools Report: What We Used in Month Two

At <a href="https://goodatscale.studio" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">GAS Studio</a>, we're always testing our tool stack. Part of the 12x12 architecture design project is evaluating which tools accelerate the process and which ones slow us down.

**SketchUp Free** continued to be the backbone for quick 3D studies. I can build a rough massing model in twenty minutes that tells me more about spatial relationships than any floor plan. For House #2, the 3D model is what showed us that the hallway scheme made the living room feel like a bowling lane — the proportions only became obvious in three dimensions.

**Rayon** got more use in month two than month one. I used it to generate initial floor plan variations when we were stuck on the narrow-lot constraint. The AI didn't solve the problem — it actually generated some of the hallway-centric plans I ended up rejecting — but it gave me a starting point to react against. Sometimes knowing what *doesn't* work is just as valuable as knowing what does.

**D5 Render** handled our visualization pass. We rendered three views of the final concept: a street view showing how the house sits on the narrow lot, a living room interior showing the uninterrupted width, and a section cut showing the relationship between the living volume and the bedroom wing.

**Pen and trace paper** — and I'm not joking — were the tools that cracked the fourth scheme. I put down the laptop, grabbed a roll of trace, and started sketching by hand. There's something about the physicality of drawing that frees up spatial thinking in a way that screen-based tools don't. This has been true every month, and I've stopped being surprised by it.

## What Fed Back Into the FOA Course

The GAS Studio 12x12 challenge exists, in part, to generate teaching material. Here's what month two contributed:

**Lesson: How to evaluate a floor plan for circulation waste.** The hallway problem in House #2 became a concrete teaching example. We now have side-by-side plans showing the same program (room count, room sizes) with and without a central hallway, and the difference in usable space is dramatic. This goes directly into the module on reading and evaluating floor plans.

**Lesson: Lot constraints are design generators.** I added a short segment to the site planning content about how narrow lots, odd shapes, and tight setbacks don't limit good design — they demand it. The best residential architecture almost always comes from constrained sites, because constraints force creativity.

**Lesson: Don't skip the section drawing.** A floor plan is a horizontal slice through a building. A section is a vertical slice. I've been guilty of treating sections as an afterthought, and House #2 punished me for it. The section revealed that the front living volume could gain two feet of ceiling height by using a shed roof instead of the flat ceiling I'd assumed. That single change transformed the feel of the main space, and I never would have found it without drawing the section.

<a href="https://www.archdaily.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">ArchDaily</a> publishes sections for almost every project they feature, and there's a reason: sections reveal what floor plans hide. The FOA Course now emphasizes this much more because of what month two taught us.

## The Emotional Side of the Residential Design Challenge

I want to be honest about something: month two was harder than month one emotionally. The novelty wore off. The first house was exciting because everything was new. The second house was a grind because the process was familiar but the problem was harder.

There's a dip in every creative project where the initial enthusiasm fades and you're left with just the work. Month two was the dip. I stared at those first three hallway schemes for longer than I should have, convinced that one more iteration would fix them. It didn't. What fixed them was accepting that the approach was wrong and starting over.

If you're planning a home and you hit a wall where nothing seems to work — that's normal. It's not a sign that your project is doomed. It's a sign that you're trying to force a solution instead of finding one. Walk away for a day. Sketch on paper. Look at the problem from a different angle — literally. Draw a section instead of a plan. Change one assumption. The breakthrough usually comes from relaxing, not from pushing harder.

## What's Coming in Month Three

Month three's GAS Studio update will cover House #3, which introduces a two-story constraint and a sloped lot. I'm already anticipating the section drawing being the hero tool again.

We're also adding a new wrinkle: each house from month three onward will get a basic energy analysis. How much solar exposure does the primary glazing get? What's the approximate heating load? This won't be a full energy model — it'll be a rough, educational calculation that shows how design decisions affect performance. It's the kind of analysis that non-architects rarely see but that dramatically affects livability and operating costs.

The whole point of the GAS Studio 12x12 challenge is to keep the design muscles working and the teaching material grounded in real practice. Theory is fine. But showing you a real house, designed under real constraints, with real mistakes and real breakthroughs — that's what makes the [FOA Course](https://foacourse.com/#pricing) different from a textbook.
