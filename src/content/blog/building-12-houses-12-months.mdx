## A Challenge That Sounds Insane (And Probably Is)

Let me tell you about a decision I made that my future self might regret: I'm designing 12 houses in 12 months.

Not building them. Not physically constructing them with my own hands — I don't hate myself that much. I'm designing them. Full residential design projects, each one exploring a different constraint, a different climate, a different set of problems. One house per month, from concept through detailed design, for an entire year.

I'm calling it the 12x12 challenge, and it's happening through <a href="https://goodatscale.studio" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">GAS Studio</a> — the design practice I run alongside the FOA Course. And yes, I know it's ambitious. That's the point.

---

## Why 12? Why Now?

Here's the honest answer: I felt stuck.

I've been teaching architectural concepts through the FOA Course for a while now. I love it. Helping people understand how homes work, how to read floor plans, how to communicate with architects — that's genuinely fulfilling. But teaching about design and actually doing design are different activities, and I started to feel the gap widening.

Every month I wasn't designing, I was one month further from the messy, frustrating, exhilarating reality of working through a residential project from scratch. And the whole premise of the FOA Course is that I translate real design experience into accessible education. Without the experience part, the education gets stale.

So I set a constraint. Twelve projects. Twelve months. Each one different enough to force me out of comfortable patterns. Not because I think twelve is a magic number, but because monthly deadlines create a rhythm that prevents me from endlessly noodling on any single project. Constraints breed creativity. That's not a bumper sticker — it's genuinely how good design happens.

---

## What Each Project Looks Like

Let me be clear about scope. These aren't napkin sketches. Each of the 12 houses in 12 months gets:

- **A real site** — an actual parcel of land with real topography, real setbacks, and real climate data
- **A real program** — a defined set of rooms, occupants, and lifestyle requirements (I'm creating fictional but realistic client profiles for each one)
- **Concept development** — massing studies, orientation analysis, spatial diagrams
- **Floor plans and elevations** — fully resolved, dimensioned architectural drawings
- **3D visualization** — rendered views showing materials, lighting, and spatial quality
- **A design narrative** — a written explanation of the design decisions, trade-offs, and lessons learned

Each project gets documented from start to finish. The process, the dead ends, the breakthroughs, the things I'd do differently — all of it.

---

## The 12 Constraints

Every project has a primary design constraint that shapes the entire approach. I haven't finalized every detail yet (the whole point is to discover things along the way), but here's the general framework:

**Months 1-3: Climate and Region.** A desert home, a cold-climate home, and a coastal home. Three completely different thermal environments, three different material palettes, three different relationships between indoor and outdoor space.

**Months 4-6: Size and Efficiency.** A micro home under 600 square feet, a family home that fits on a narrow urban lot, and an ADU designed to maximize independence on a constrained site. These three are all about doing more with less.

**Months 7-9: Lifestyle and Program.** A multigenerational home, a live-work home for a creative professional, and a home designed for aging in place. Each one puts a specific set of human needs at the center of the design process.

**Months 10-12: Ideas and Experimentation.** A passive house targeting net-zero energy, a home built primarily from one material system, and a wildcard project that I'll define based on whatever I've learned in the first nine months. These final three are where I push into unfamiliar territory.

## How This Feeds Into the Course

Here's where you come in. If you're an FOA Course student — or thinking about becoming one — the 12x12 challenge isn't just a personal project. It's a content engine.

Every design decision I make during these projects is a teaching opportunity. When I'm agonizing over whether to orient the living room east or south on the desert house, that's a real lesson about solar exposure and passive cooling. When I'm trying to fit a functional kitchen into a 550-square-foot micro home, that's a practical demonstration of space planning principles.

I'm planning to document each project's key lessons and fold them directly into the FOA Course materials. If you've read our <a href="https://foacourse.com/blog/foa-course-module-breakdown">module breakdown</a>, you know the course covers everything from site analysis to construction documents. The 12x12 projects will provide fresh, real-world examples for nearly every module.

Some of what I produce will show up as case studies within the course. Some will become standalone blog posts (like our <a href="https://foacourse.com/blog/month-one-building-foa-course">month one behind-the-scenes post</a>). And some will live on the GAS Studio site as portfolio pieces that demonstrate the design thinking I teach.

The goal is to close the loop between teaching and doing. Every project makes me a better teacher. Every teaching moment clarifies what the next project should explore.

## What I Hope to Learn

I'm not approaching this as an expert executing flawless designs. I'm approaching it as a designer deliberately putting himself in uncomfortable situations to learn faster. Here's what I'm hoping to get out of it:

**Speed and decisiveness.** When you have a month instead of a year, you can't afford to be precious. You make decisions, commit, and keep moving. This is actually how real residential projects work for most clients — timelines are tight, budgets are real, and perfect is the enemy of done.

**Range.** Designing the same type of house over and over builds depth but not breadth. By forcing myself across climates, scales, and programs, I'm building a more versatile design vocabulary. That directly benefits FOA Course students because the principles I teach become more universal.

**Humility about what I don't know.** Every project will expose gaps in my knowledge. The coastal home will force me to learn about corrosion-resistant materials and flood mitigation. The passive house will push me deeper into building science and energy modeling. I'd rather discover these gaps in a design exercise than in a real client project.

**Better visual communication.** Each project requires me to produce clear, compelling drawings and renderings. The more I practice this, the better I get at explaining spatial concepts visually — which is exactly what the FOA Course demands.

## The Part That Scares Me

I'll be honest: publishing design work monthly, in public, with a written narrative explaining every decision — that's exposing. There will be projects where the design doesn't quite come together. There will be floor plans where someone smarter than me points out a circulation problem I missed. There will be renderings where the proportions feel slightly off and I'll know it but ship it anyway because the deadline doesn't care about my perfectionism.

That's the whole point. The residential design challenge isn't about producing twelve perfect projects. It's about producing twelve honest projects and learning from each one in real time. <a href="https://www.archdaily.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">ArchDaily</a> publishes projects from the world's best firms, and even those have compromises and trade-offs. Design is never finished — it's abandoned at the point where the most important problems have been solved.

If I waited until I was confident every detail was flawless, I'd never publish anything. And more importantly, I'd never generate the real-world design lessons that make the FOA Course genuinely useful.

## Following Along

Here's how you can track the 12x12 challenge:

- **GAS Studio** will host the full project documentation — drawings, renderings, narratives, and process shots
- **The FOA blog** will feature behind-the-scenes posts for each project, focusing on the lessons most relevant to people planning their own homes
- **The FOA Course** will incorporate key learnings as they emerge, keeping the curriculum connected to live design work

I'll be sharing early sketches, dead-end ideas that didn't work, and the final resolved designs. The messy middle is where the most useful lessons live, and I'm committed to showing it.

---

## Constraints Are the Point

If there's one thing I want you to take away from this, it's that constraints aren't obstacles — they're the raw material of good design. A tight lot, a modest budget, a challenging climate, a strict timeline — these aren't problems to overcome. They're the forces that push a design from generic to specific, from acceptable to genuinely good.

The 12 houses in 12 months challenge is my way of proving that to myself and demonstrating it for you. Each project is a constraint-driven design exercise that tests real principles in real conditions.

Whether you follow every project or just catch the highlights, I hope it makes one thing clear: you don't need an unlimited budget or a perfect site to design something worth building. You need clear thinking, good fundamentals, and the willingness to work within your limits.
