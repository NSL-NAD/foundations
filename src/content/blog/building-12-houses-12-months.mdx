## 12 Projects. 12 Months. One Rule: Ship It.

At the start of this year I gave myself a challenge through <a href="https://goodatscale.studio" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">GAS Studio</a>: launch 12 projects in 12 months.

Not plan 12 projects. Not brainstorm 12 projects. Launch them. Put them out into the world where people can actually use them.

I'm calling it the 12x12 challenge, and it's the most ambitious thing I've committed to publicly. The projects span apps, e-commerce, education, marketing — anything where modern tools and technology give a solo creator the leverage to ship something real. The theme behind all of it: doing more good, at scale.

---

## Why 12x12?

The honest answer is that I was tired of having ideas and not executing on them.

I've spent years learning about design, architecture, technology, and business. I have notebooks full of project ideas, some of them genuinely good. But the gap between having an idea and shipping something people can touch is enormous — and it grows wider every month you don't cross it.

So I set a constraint. Twelve launches. Twelve months. Each project different enough to push me into unfamiliar territory. Not because twelve is a magic number, but because monthly deadlines create a rhythm. They prevent you from endlessly polishing, endlessly planning, endlessly convincing yourself you need "just one more week."

The tools available today make this possible in a way it wouldn't have been even a few years ago. AI-assisted development, no-code platforms, modern web frameworks, on-demand e-commerce fulfillment — the barriers to launching are lower than ever. The 12x12 is my way of testing how far one person can push that advantage.

---

## What's Launched So Far

Four projects are live as of this writing:

**1. Margle** — A web application. This was the first 12x12 project and my trial by fire. Building and shipping a functional app in a single month taught me more about prioritization than years of planning ever did. The core lesson: figure out the one thing the product must do well, build that, and launch.

**2. FOA Course** — The Foundations of Architecture Course you're reading this on. This was by far the most complex 12x12 project. A full education platform with a 62-lesson curriculum, student dashboard, Stripe checkout, and a blog publishing system. Building it under the 12x12 deadline forced decisions that would have taken months in a normal timeline — and honestly, most of those fast decisions turned out fine.

**3. GAS Studio Website** — The design practice needed a home. Instead of agonizing over every detail for six months, the 12x12 constraint forced a clean, functional launch. The lesson here: a live website you can improve is infinitely more valuable than a perfect website that lives in Figma.

**4. GAS Merch Store** — An e-commerce project. Setting up a brand, designing products, configuring fulfillment, and launching a store — all within the monthly cadence. This one pushed me deepest into territory I had no prior experience in, which is exactly the point.

Eight more projects are planned. Some are further apps, some are marketing experiments, some are creative projects I'm not ready to announce yet. You can follow the full lineup at <a href="https://goodatscale.studio" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">goodatscale.studio</a>.

## What the 12x12 Is Teaching Me

Four projects in and the lessons are stacking up fast:

**Done beats perfect.** This isn't new advice, but living it monthly makes it visceral. Every project has launched with things I'd love to fix. Every single one. And every single one is better for being live than it would be sitting in a staging environment waiting for perfection.

**Modern tools are a genuine multiplier.** AI-assisted coding, component libraries, managed hosting, automated workflows — the stack available to solo creators today is extraordinary. The 12x12 is partly an experiment in how much leverage these tools actually provide when you commit to using them aggressively.

**Diverse projects build transferable skills.** The e-commerce lessons from the merch store informed how I think about the FOA Course checkout flow. The app development discipline from Margle made the course platform build smoother. Skills compound across domains in ways you can't predict until you're doing the work.

**Shipping is a muscle.** The first launch was terrifying. The fourth was still uncomfortable but manageable. There's a mental shift that happens when you commit to a cadence — you stop asking "is this ready?" and start asking "what's the minimum viable version I'm proud enough to ship?"

## How This Connects to FOA

You might be wondering why an architecture education blog is talking about launching apps and merch stores. Fair question.

The FOA Course was one of the 12x12 projects. The same discipline, pace, and "ship it" mentality that drives the challenge is what made the course happen. Without the 12x12 deadline, I'd probably still be reorganizing the curriculum outline.

But the connection goes deeper than that. The 12x12 is fundamentally about using constraints as creative fuel — which is the exact same principle that drives good architectural design. A tight lot, a modest budget, a challenging timeline: these aren't obstacles, they're the forces that push work from generic to genuinely good.

Everything I'm learning from shipping these projects feeds back into how I teach. The module on <a href="https://foacourse.com/blog/foa-course-module-breakdown">design development</a> benefits from my recent experience making fast decisions under real deadlines. The curriculum's emphasis on practical over theoretical gets sharper every time I launch something and discover what actually matters versus what I thought would matter.

Building in public like this also keeps me honest. If I'm teaching people how to think about design and planning, I should be actively doing both — not just lecturing from past experience.

---

## The Philosophy: Doing More Good, At Scale

GAS Studio stands for Good At Scale. The idea behind it is simple: if you have skills and tools and time, you should be putting things into the world. Not hoarding ideas. Not waiting for permission. Not perfecting in private.

The 12x12 is the purest expression of that philosophy. Twelve projects means twelve opportunities to create something useful, solve a real problem, or make something that didn't exist before. Some will succeed. Some won't. But all of them will be out there, which is more than most ideas ever achieve.

The tools and technology available right now make it possible for small teams — even individuals — to ship at a pace that used to require entire companies. The 12x12 is my way of testing that thesis with my own time and energy.

## Your Version of 12x12

You don't have to launch 12 projects to benefit from this mindset. The core idea — set a constraint, commit publicly, ship on a deadline — works at any scale.

Maybe it's one home renovation decision per week instead of agonizing for months. Maybe it's finishing your floor plan sketches this month instead of next quarter. Maybe it's finally reaching out to that architect you've been meaning to contact.

The 12x12 taught me that the biggest obstacle to doing good work isn't skill or resources — it's the gap between intention and action. Constraints close that gap.
