## Not Everyone Learns the Same Way

When I started building Foundations of Architecture, I ran into a problem almost immediately. The people signing up fell into two very distinct camps.

Some wanted to pick up a pencil, grab some trace paper, and start sketching floor plans by hand. They think spatially. They doodle on napkins. They want to *draw* their dream home into existence.

Others had zero interest in picking up a pencil. They wanted to organize their ideas, build a comprehensive plan, and hand a polished document to an architect or builder. They think in systems. They make spreadsheets. They want to *brief* their dream home into existence.

Both approaches are completely valid. So instead of forcing everyone down one path, FOA Course offers two: The Drawer and The Brief Builder.

---

## The Drawer Path: Pencil, Paper, Spatial Thinking

The Drawer path is for people who learn by making. Every module includes hands-on drawing exercises — from basic line-weight practice to full floor plan sketches at scale.

You don't need any artistic talent. Seriously. Architectural drawing is a technical skill, not an artistic one. If you can draw a straight line with a ruler (and sometimes even that's optional), you have what it takes.

Here's what The Drawer path looks like in practice:

- **You sketch your own floor plans** using the drawing fundamentals covered in Module 3. You'll learn line weights, symbols, and how to draw rooms at scale.
- **You draw elevations** — the front, back, and side views of your home — to explore what it looks like from the outside.
- **You create sections** that cut through your home vertically, showing ceiling heights, floor levels, and how spaces stack.
- **You develop a materials palette** by hand, sketching textures and annotating finishes you want to use.

By the end of the course, Drawers have a physical portfolio: hand-drawn floor plans, elevations, sections, and a materials board. It's the kind of package that makes an architect's day because it shows them exactly how you think about your home.

The Drawer path works especially well if you're the kind of person who needs to see something take shape on paper before it feels real. There's a directness to sketching that no digital tool can replicate — your hand, your eye, your decisions, no software between you and the idea.

---

## The Brief Builder Path: Digital, Organized, Document-Driven

The Brief Builder path is for people who think in words, lists, and structured documents. Instead of drawing your dream home, you *describe* it — in precise, organized detail.

A design brief is the document you hand to an architect that says: here's what I want, here's why, here's my budget, here are my priorities, and here's how I want to live. A great brief is worth its weight in gold because it prevents the back-and-forth that burns time and money during the design process.

Here's what The Brief Builder path looks like:

- **You compile a vision statement** that captures the feeling and values behind your dream home — not just what it looks like, but what it means.
- **You build room-by-room requirements** with specific spatial needs, adjacencies, and priority levels. This is where answering those [seven pre-design questions](/blog/7-questions-before-designing-dream-home) really pays off.
- **You create a materials and finishes document** using reference images, links, and written descriptions instead of hand-drawn samples.
- **You assemble a site analysis** with orientation preferences, outdoor space requirements, and environmental considerations.

By the end, Brief Builders have a comprehensive design brief — a polished PDF document they can hand to any architect or builder and say "this is what I want." It's organized, thorough, and eliminates the guesswork that typically plagues the early phases of a home project.

The Brief Builder path works best if you're more comfortable in Google Docs than on graph paper, or if you already know you'll hire a professional and want to give them the clearest possible starting point.

## Same Foundation, Different Expression

Here's what matters: both paths cover the same core material. Every student learns the same design principles, the same spatial thinking frameworks, and the same vocabulary.

The difference is in the exercises and the final deliverable. Drawers express their understanding through sketches. Brief Builders express it through structured documents. But the architectural knowledge underneath is identical.

You'll learn about proportion, scale, and light — whether you're sketching a window or writing a specification for one. You'll understand circulation and flow — whether you're drawing arrows on a floor plan or describing movement patterns in a brief. Sites like [ArchDaily](https://www.archdaily.com) and [Dwell](https://www.dwell.com) are full of projects that started both ways.

And honestly? Some students do both. They sketch when a concept is fuzzy and they need to think it through spatially, then switch to writing when they need to lock down the details. The course is designed so you can move between paths whenever it makes sense.

## Which Path Should You Choose?

Don't overthink this. Ask yourself one question:

**When you imagine your dream home, do you see it or describe it?**

If you close your eyes and see rooms, shapes, light falling across a floor — start with The Drawer. Your brain already works in images.

If you close your eyes and hear yourself saying "I want the kitchen to be open to the living room with a big island and a view of the backyard" — start with The Brief Builder. Your brain works in language and relationships.

There's no wrong answer. Both paths lead to the same outcome: a clear, detailed vision of your dream home that you can use to communicate with professionals, make confident decisions, and avoid the expensive mistakes that come from not knowing what you want.
