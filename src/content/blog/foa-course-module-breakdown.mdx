## What's Actually Inside This Course?

I get this question a lot — and honestly, I'd ask it too. Ninety-three dollars isn't nothing. Before you hand over your money, you deserve to know exactly what you're getting, what you'll learn, and whether it's built for someone like you.

So here's the full, transparent walkthrough of every section in Foundations of Architecture. No vague promises. No marketing fluff. Just a clear breakdown of what the FOA Course covers, module by module, so you can decide if it's the right fit.

The course is structured as a journey: you start with big-picture thinking, build foundational skills in the middle, and finish with a portfolio project that ties everything together. There are 106 lessons across 10 sections — a mix of video, text, hands-on exercises, and downloadable resources. You work at your own pace. There are no deadlines, no cohorts, no pressure.

Let's walk through it.

---

## Welcome & Orientation

**3 lessons | ~11 minutes**

This section gets you set up. You'll meet me (hi), understand how the course is structured, and choose your learning path. The FOA Course offers two tracks — one for people who want to learn architecture broadly, and one for people actively designing a specific home. Both tracks cover the same material, but the exercises are tailored differently.

You'll also get a materials checklist so you know exactly what tools and supplies you need. Spoiler: it's minimal. No expensive software required.

---

## Kickoff: Dream Home Discovery

**4 lessons | ~60 minutes**

Before we touch a single architectural concept, we start with *you*. This section is about defining what "dream home" actually means for your specific life — not someone else's Pinterest board.

You'll create a mood board, write a vision statement, and work through a set of inspiration questions designed to surface what you actually care about in a home. These exercises produce the raw material that drives every design decision later in the course. If you've read my article on [7 questions to answer before designing your dream home](https://foacourse.com/blog/7-questions-before-designing-dream-home), this section goes much deeper into that same territory.

Most people tell me this is where the course first surprises them. It feels like a design exercise, but it's really a self-discovery exercise. And it makes everything that follows more personal and more useful.

---

## Module 1: Architecture as an Idea

**7 lessons | ~63 minutes**

Now we zoom out. What *is* architecture, really? Not the technical definition — the philosophical one. Why do some spaces make you feel calm and others make you anxious? Why have certain design principles persisted for 2,000 years?

This module covers the Vitruvian principles (firmness, commodity, delight — the original architecture framework from ancient Rome) and how modern architects have expanded on them. You'll study inspiration from the masters — architects like Frank Lloyd Wright, Tadao Ando, and Zaha Hadid — not to copy them, but to understand the *thinking* behind great design.

The module ends with an exercise where you articulate your own design philosophy. This sounds grand, but it's practical: it gives you a clear framework for making hundreds of small decisions later in the process. Institutions like <a href="https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/architecture" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">MIT's architecture program</a> build entire semesters around this kind of foundational thinking. We compress the essential parts into something you can work through in an afternoon.

---

## Module 2: Definitions of Architecture

**16 lessons | ~118 minutes**

This is the vocabulary module, and it's the biggest section of the course for good reason. You can't participate meaningfully in the design process if you don't speak the language.

We cover every fundamental architectural element: space, form, proportion, scale, light and shadow, texture and materiality, rhythm, symmetry, context, structure, transitions, and color. Each element gets its own lesson with real-world examples, clear explanations, and visual references.

If you've read my blog posts on [architectural scale](https://foacourse.com/blog/what-is-architectural-scale) or [universal design](https://foacourse.com/blog/what-is-universal-design), those are samples of the depth we go into here — but the course versions are more comprehensive and include interactive exercises.

The module wraps with an Element Preferences exercise where you identify which architectural elements matter most to you personally. This isn't just academic — it becomes a decision-making tool you'll use throughout the rest of the course and, eventually, in conversations with your architect.

---

## Module 3: Drawing Foundations

**14 lessons | ~174 minutes**

Here's where this architecture course online gets hands-on. You'll learn to read — and create — the four fundamental architectural drawings: site plans, floor plans, elevations, and sections. No prior drawing experience needed. We start from zero.

Beyond drawing types, this module covers flow and circulation (how people move through spaces), storage design (the most underrated topic in residential architecture), buildability basics, zoning and code fundamentals, room-specific design principles, and a budget checkpoint that connects your design aspirations to financial reality.

The capstone exercise is creating a Space Requirements Document — essentially a detailed program that describes every room in your dream home, its size, its adjacencies, and its requirements. This is the document that architects love to receive from clients because it shows you've done the thinking.

---

## Module 4: Materiality & Systems

**18 lessons | ~115 minutes**

Materials are where architecture stops being abstract and becomes tangible. This module covers the major building materials — concrete, wood, steel, glass, stone, brick, engineered timber, copper, tile, plaster, composites — explaining what each does well, where each fails, and how they work together in a building system.

We also cover sustainable materials, building systems integration (how HVAC, plumbing, electrical, and structural systems interact), and construction and finishing processes. There's a budget checkpoint here too, because material choices are one of the biggest cost drivers in any project.

The module ends with a Materials Preferences exercise where you build a personal materials palette — a curated selection of materials that reflect your aesthetic, your values, and your budget. Related reading: our article on [biophilic design](https://foacourse.com/blog/what-is-biophilic-design) explores how natural material choices affect your relationship with a space.

---

## Module 5: Environmental Design

**10 lessons | ~91 minutes**

This module covers how your home interacts with its environment — sunlight, wind, earth, and energy. You'll learn about solar orientation (why which direction your house faces matters enormously), natural ventilation strategies, insulation and thermal mass, and energy systems.

If you've read my article on [passive solar design](https://foacourse.com/blog/what-is-passive-solar-design), this module expands on those concepts significantly. We also cover site response and harmony — how to design a home that works *with* its specific piece of land rather than against it — and the interplay of texture, light, and sound in creating comfortable interior environments.

The module produces a Site Requirements Document that captures your environmental priorities and site-specific observations. Combined with the Space Requirements Document from Module 3, you now have a serious design brief taking shape.

---

## Module 6: Portfolio Project

**11 lessons | ~198 minutes**

Everything comes together here. This is the capstone — a guided portfolio project where you create a comprehensive design package for your dream home.

Step by step, you'll produce: a vision statement, a site plan, a floor plan, elevations, a materials board, an environmental diagram, and a final compiled design brief. Each step builds on the knowledge and exercises from previous modules.

The final compilation is something you can literally hand to an architect and say, "This is what I want." It demonstrates design thinking, communicates specific preferences, and shows that you've done the foundational work. Architects genuinely appreciate receiving this kind of preparation from clients — it saves everyone time and leads to better outcomes.

## Bonus Module: AI for Architecture

**11 lessons | ~125 minutes**

This is the section I'm most excited about. AI tools have fundamentally changed what non-architects can do with design visualization, and this module teaches you how to use them.

We cover the AI tool landscape, then do hands-on deep dives into specific platforms: Rayon for floor plans, Spacely AI for interior visualization, Google AI Studio for concept imagery, and D5 Render for photorealistic output. There are dedicated lessons on prompting for architecture (getting AI to produce what you actually want) and integrating AI into your design workflow. If you've read our [best AI tools for home design](https://foacourse.com/blog/best-ai-tools-home-design-2026) article, this module is the full course version.

The module ends with a major exercise: using AI tools to visualize the dream home you've been designing throughout the course. <a href="https://www.archdaily.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">ArchDaily</a> has been covering how professional firms use these exact tools — now you can use them yourself.
